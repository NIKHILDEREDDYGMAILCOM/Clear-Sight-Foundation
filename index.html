The Basics

What is Myopia?

Myopia, commonly called nearsightedness, is a refractive error that makes distant objects appear blurry while close objects remain clear. It occurs when the eyeball grows too long or the cornea curves too steeply.

Myopia typically begins in childhood and often progresses through the teenage years. While glasses and contact lenses correct vision, they don't stop its progression. High myopia increases the risk of serious eye disease later in life — including retinal detachment and glaucoma.

🔬

Refractive Error

Light focuses in front of the retina instead of directly on it, causing distant images to appear blurry.

📏

Axial Elongation

The eye grows longer than normal — even 1mm of extra length can cause significant vision change.

⚠️

High Myopia Risk

Prescriptions above –6.00 diopters significantly raise the risk of sight-threatening complications.

📈

Progressive Condition

Myopia often worsens year after year during school age, making early intervention critical.

Risk Factors

What Causes Myopia?

Myopia is influenced by both genetics and environment. The modern lifestyle — more screen time, less outdoor activity — has accelerated its rise dramatically.

01

Genetics

If one parent has myopia, a child has a 3× greater risk. With both parents myopic, the risk rises to 6×. Over 200 genes have been linked to myopia.

02

Near Work & Screens

Extended time on phones, tablets, and computers — especially in poor lighting — forces the eyes into sustained close focus, stressing development.

03

Lack of Outdoor Time

Natural light stimulates dopamine release in the retina, which slows eye elongation. Children who spend less time outdoors are significantly more at risk.

04

Urbanization

Urban children have dramatically higher myopia rates than rural peers. Dense built environments limit natural light exposure and long-distance viewing.

05

Educational Pressure

Countries with intense academic cultures show higher rates. Countries like Singapore and South Korea see 80–90% myopia rates in young adults.

06

Age of Onset

Earlier onset (under age 7) is a strong predictor of high myopia by adulthood. Progression is fastest during school years.

Signs to Watch For

Recognizing the Symptoms

Many children don't realize their vision is abnormal — they've never seen any other way. Knowing the signs helps catch myopia early.

Squinting to see distant objects or the board at school
Sitting closer to screens or the front of classrooms
Frequent headaches or eye strain after class
Rubbing eyes more than usual
Difficulty seeing in low-light or at dusk
Losing interest in activities that require distance vision
Holding books and devices unusually close to the face
Complaints of blurry vision when looking far away

What You Can Do

Prevention & Management

While genetics can't be changed, lifestyle choices profoundly affect whether and how severely myopia develops. Small daily habits make a real difference.

Outdoor Time

Spend 2 Hours Outside Daily

Research consistently shows that at least 90–120 minutes of outdoor time per day can significantly slow or even prevent myopia onset in children. Natural light is the key factor.

Screen Habits

Follow the 20-20-20 Rule

Every 20 minutes of near work, look at something 20 feet away for 20 seconds. This gives your eye muscles a critical break and reduces strain-related progression risk.

Lighting

Read in Good Lighting

Poor lighting forces the eyes to work harder. Study and read in well-lit spaces — natural light is ideal. Avoid reading lying down or in dim environments.

Eye Exams

Annual Comprehensive Eye Exams

Children should have annual eye exams, especially if myopia is detected. Early intervention with myopia control treatments is most effective when started young.

Distance

Maintain Proper Working Distance

Hold books and devices at arm's length (at least 30cm/12 inches). Don't hunch over screens. Good posture and proper desk setup reduce near-work strain.

Diet

Nutrition Matters

A diet rich in Vitamin D (sunlight, fish, eggs) and antioxidants supports eye health. Some studies link low Vitamin D levels to higher myopia risk.

Medical Options

Treatment Approaches

Beyond glasses and contacts, modern myopia control treatments can actively slow progression — the goal is not just clear vision today, but healthy eyes for life.

Find an Eye Doctor

Eyeglasses & Contact Lenses

Standard correction for daily clear vision. Single-vision lenses correct the prescription but don't slow progression. Bifocal or progressive lenses may offer modest control.

Orthokeratology (Ortho-K)

Specially designed rigid lenses worn overnight temporarily reshape the cornea, providing clear daytime vision without glasses while also slowing axial elongation significantly.

Atropine Eye Drops

Low-dose atropine drops (0.01–0.05%) are clinically proven to slow myopia progression by up to 50% with minimal side effects. Applied nightly under medical supervision.

Multifocal Contact Lenses

Designed to reduce peripheral retinal defocus that drives eye elongation. FDA-approved myopia control soft lenses show significant slowing effect in clinical trials.

LASIK / Refractive Surgery

Permanent surgical correction for adults (18+) with stable prescriptions. Corrects vision but does not reduce underlying risk for myopia-related complications in high myopia.

About This Project

Why We Started This

This website is a high school passion project dedicated to spreading awareness about the growing myopia epidemic. After noticing how many of our classmates needed glasses — and how few understood why or what to do about it — we decided to do something.

We believe that access to clear, evidence-based information can change outcomes. Vision affects academic performance, mental health, and quality of life — yet myopia is still widely misunderstood as a simple inconvenience rather than a manageable health issue.

"The eye is the lamp of the body. Good vision isn't a given — it's something we protect together."

This is a student-led awareness project. For medical advice, please consult a licensed eye care professional.
